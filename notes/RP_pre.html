General Introduction

Motivation

In the journey of life, education plays a pivotal role in shaping the trajectory of individuals, from their early stages in school to their later stages as experienced workers.

Understanding the dynamic relationship between education, human capital development, labor market outcomes, and economic growth is crucial for comprehending the broader implications of educational systems on individuals and societies across the life cycle.

1    Does Raising Costs Boost Student Effort?

Background: what has happened in the real world?

The amount of per-student spending in HE tops the education system around the world; HE fundings keeps rising at a faster pace than the increase in enrolment rates.


However, the use of these funds is not as efficient as expected: in OECD countries, only 39% graduate on time and over 1/5 do not graduate after all (OECD, 2022).


Systems are different around the world: more enrolment less funding, less enrolment more funding? Or even more enrolment more funding?


This is particularly relevant in the context of limited funding resources. For instance, during the European debt crisis, the Spanish government reduced funding for higher education as a measure to decrease its overall expenditure, resulting in an increase in tuition fees paid by university students.

Question: what do we want to understand? and why?

There has been an ongoing debate in policy practice or theoretical research on whether public universities should charge fees, to what extent the state should provide financial support for students, and what portion of the cost of higher education should be borne by individuals.


How can tuition fees be adjusted to make the financial investment in higher education more efficient? Or, will higher or lower tuition fees motivate students to use higher educational resources more efficiently, resulting in better academic performance? If so, to what extent?

Identification Strategy (1): the "participation" specification

Staggered diffrence-in-diffrerence: \[ y_{ics}=\sum_c\alpha_c+\sum_s\gamma_s+\beta D_{ics}+\theta X_i+\varepsilon_{ics}, \] where $\alpha_c$ and $\gamma_s$ are dummies that capture the cohort and year-of-study FEs respectively, $y_{ics}$ is the outcome of individual $i$ in the $k$-th year of study (e.g., average of credits taken, course passing rate, participation dummy), and $D_{ics}$ is a dummy that takes 1 if the cohort of the individual is exposed to the rise in the $k$-th year of study.

Concern: Non-random attrition may lead to bias, since we are reasoning with cohorts.

Solution: $ y_{is}=\sum_i\alpha_i+\sum_s\gamma_s+\beta D_{is}+\varepsilon_{is}, $
                where $\alpha_i$ captures the individual FE.

Identification Strategy (2): the "performance" specification

Staggered diffrence-in-diffrerence: \[ y_{ijt}=\sum_i\alpha_i+\sum_j\gamma_j+\sum_t \delta_t+\beta D_{it}+\varepsilon_{ijt}. \] where $\alpha_i$ and $\gamma_j$ are dummies that capture the individual, course (module), and time FEs respectively, $y_{ijs}$ is the outcome of individual $i$ in the course $j$ at year $t$ (e.g., grade, pass or not), and $D_{it}$ is a dummy that takes 1 if the cohort of the individual is exposed to the rise in the academic year $t$.
