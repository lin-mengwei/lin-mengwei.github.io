General Introduction

Motivation

In the journey of life, education plays a pivotal role in shaping the trajectory of individuals, from early days in school to later stages as experienced workers. Understanding the interplay among education attainment, human capital development, and different aspects of labor market outcomes is crucial for comprehending the implications of educational systems on both individuals and societies.

General Introduction

Overview

In this PhD dissertation, I will explore three interconnected facets that span different stages of life, examining student incentives during university years and extending to labor market risks faced by individuals in later stages of their careers.

1    Does Raising Costs Boost Student Effort?

Identification Strategy (1): the "student-year" specification

Staggered diffrence-in-diffrerence: \[ y_{ics}=\sum_{c=2007}^{2017}\alpha_c+\sum_{s=1}^4\gamma_s+\beta D_{ics}+\theta X_i+\varepsilon_{ics}, \]
  • $\alpha_c$ and $\gamma_s$: cohort ($c$) and year-of-study ($s$) Fixed Effects
  • $y_{ics}$: outcome of student $i$ in the $k$-th year of study (e.g., average of credits taken, course passing rate, participation dummy)
  • $D_{ics}$: treatment dummy taking 1 if $i$ in $c$ exposed to the rise in the $k$-th year of study

Concern: Non-random attrition may lead to bias, since we are reasoning with cohorts.

Solution: $ y_{is}=\sum_i\alpha_i+\sum_s\gamma_s+\beta D_{is}+\varepsilon_{is}, $
                where $\alpha_i$ captures the individual FE.

2   ‚ÄúGood good study, safe safe job?‚Äù:
      Education and Risks in Labor Market Performance in China

Motivation: "Study well now, and find a nice stable job later."

  • Theory side: In the field of human capital investment, the estimation of returns is at the center of the stage, leaving risks disproportionately unexplored.
  • Context side:
    1. A rapid change in the socio-economic conditions: the government relinquished its role as the primary risk bearer, passing the responsibility onto market players.
    2. A culturally-rooted attitude of risk aversion: from both the traditional Confucianism and the recent traumas (Japanese Invasion, civil war, famines)

Literature on the risk aspect of HC investment

  • Adjusting rate of returns to education by taking risks into account (e.g., Weiss, 1972; Olson et al., 1979; Hartog and Diaz-Serrano, 2014; Delaney, 2019;)
  • Empirically estimating causality between risks in labor market performance and eduction (Delaney and Devereux, 2019)

2   ‚ÄúGood good study, safe safe job?‚Äù:
      Education and Risks in Labor Market Performance in China

Question and Hypothesis

  • How does education affect risks in labor market performance (measured by income volatility and duration of job)?
  • How does this relationship behave as levels of education change? (Does more education monotonically lead to jobs with lower risks?)

Identification Strategy and Data

  • To examine the possibly non-linear relationship, I plan to use two policy changes as IVs:
    1. IV #1: the Compulsory Education Law (compliers shifted from low to middle level of education)
    2. IV #2: the Expansion of Higher Education (compliers shifted from middle to high level of education)
  • Data: Life History Survey, China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (2014, 20,543 obs); the China Labor-force Dynamics Survey (2012 to 2018, 20,000+ obs).

3   Make Gray the New Green:
      Human Capital Re-Investment in Older Workers

Background: the aging society

  • Population aging has become the new norm of the world, albeit at varying rates across different regions. Failure to deal with it may lead to conflicts in the distribution of resources, economic recessions, and social instability. (Goodhart and Pradhan, 2020; Lee, 2016; National Academy of Sciences, 2012)

Motivation: being old ‚â† being a social burden

  • Current explorations from the policy side have been based more on ideas of increasing the consumption of the elderly and reducing the burden of elderly care. (European Commission et al., 2018; Prince et al., 2015; Tang et al., 2022).
  • Such approaches are necessary but insufficient, as they are treating the elderly as unproductive piggy banks or social burdens.
  • Older workers can continue to contribute to socio-economic development, only with appropriate measures in place (e.g., see OECD (2019))

3   Make Gray the New Green:

Research Question, Identification Strategy and Data

  • Question: Will targeting training improve the labor market situations of older workers? How much do on-the-job trainings increase wages for older workers and helps them stay longer in the labor market?
  • Identification: To exploit training programs provided through the WeGebAU (Further Training for Low Skilled and Older Employees in Companies) in Germany since 2006.
  • Data from the Research Data Center (FDZ) of the German Federal Employment Agency (BA) at the Institute for Employment Research (lAB).

Literature and Contribution

  • Active Labor Market Policies affecting older workers: Morris and Caro (1995), Samorodov (1999), Card et al. (2018)
  • Training and Education targeting older workers: Picchio (2021), Midtsundstad (2019), Dauth and Toomet (2016), Belloni and Villosio (2015), Picchio and van Ours (2013)

Identification Strategy (2): the "performance" specification

Staggered diffrence-in-diffrerence: \[ y_{ijt}=\sum_i\alpha_i+\sum_j\gamma_j+\sum_t \delta_t+\beta D_{it}+\varepsilon_{ijt}. \] where $\alpha_i$ and $\gamma_j$ are dummies that capture the individual, course (module), and time FEs respectively, $y_{ijs}$ is the outcome of individual $i$ in the course $j$ at year $t$ (e.g., grade, pass or not), and $D_{it}$ is a dummy that takes 1 if the cohort of the individual is exposed to the rise in the academic year $t$.
