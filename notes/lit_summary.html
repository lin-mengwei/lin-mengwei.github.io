Literature Summary: Retake Policy Project







I. On Tuition Fees

1. Bietenbeck et al. (2023). Tuition fees and educational attainment. European Economic Review.
  • Question: Do university tuition fees affect educational attainment?
  • Broader Question: tuition fees → academic outcomes
  • Policy change: German universities started to charge tuition fees after 2005, which was abolished during the later decade
  • Outcomes: degree completion within 6 years, enrollment; grades, study time
  • Data Source: 1) admin data: German Student Register & Final Examinations Register (cross-sectional at individual level; panel at cohort level)
    2) survey data: 3 waves of Student Survey conducted between 2004 and 2010 (panel, individual level)
  • Sample: students initially enrolling at university between 1995 and 2004 in nine states (731,352 students)
  • Identification: Difference in Difference - comparing universities charging a fee with those not before and after 2005
  • Conclusion: Overal effect of charging fees is insignificant:
    1) [at the intensive margin] increasing students effort and degree completion, while
    2) [at the extensive margin] decreasing university enrollment rates.
2. Filipa Sa. (2019). The Effect of University Fees on Applications, Attendance and Course Choice: Evidence from a Natural Experiment in the UK. Economica.
  • Question: How do tuition fees affect university applications, attendance and course choice?
  • Broader Question: tuition fees → academic behavoirs
  • Policy Change: the increase in fees in England from £3,375 to a maximum of £9,000 per year in 2012 (not uniformly across all UK countries)
  • Outcomes: applications, attendance, course choice
  • Data Source: admin data: applications by students (from UCAS); number of freshmen at universities and salaries and employment rates after graduation (from HESA).
  • Sample: applications (98,691) and enrolment (71,711), 2008 to 2015 in England and Scotland
  • Identification: Difference in Difference - English vs Scotland before and after the 2012 change
  • Conclusion: The increase in fees reduced applications by 18.7% and the number of first-year undergraduates by 10.9%.
  • Heterogeneity:
    1) larger effects for courses with lower expected salaries or employment rates
    2) smaller effects for STEM courses and selective universities
    3) larger effects for richer (credit constriant not key here), white, and female students
  • Side notes: 1) Estimates of price elasticity of demand (application) for university (-0.4).
    2)* Cannot untangle the effect of fees from that of funding policy changes during that time.
3. Hans Fricke. (2018). Tuition Fees, Student Finances, and Student Achievement: Evidence from a Differential Raise in Fees. JHC
  • Question: How does students finance an unexpected increase in tuition fees at a Swiss university and how do the effects on student finances translate into effects on on-time degree completion and grades?
  • Broader Question: tuition fees → academic performance
  • Policy Change: In the spring of 2012, the University of St. Gallen unexpectedly raised tuition fees for all students (CHF950 or 80% for foreigners and CHF200 or 20% for Swiss students per semester), including those who were already enrolled.
  • Outcomes: spending, income source, on-time degree completion, credit accumulation, GPA
  • Data Source: survey data from Social and Economic Conditions of Student Life Survey; admin data of student records of the University of St. Gallen.
  • Sample: students who successfully completed the first year in St. Gallen and enrolled before Spring 2011 (for academic outcomes)
  • Identification: Difference in Difference - Swiss and foreign students before and after
  • Conclusion: 1) Students do not take on more loans; they seem to almost entirely compensate for the fees by reducing consumption spending.
    2) There was no significant effects of the increase on on-time graduation, credit accumulation, or GPA.
  • Side notes:
4. Garibaldi et al. (2012). College cost and time to complete a degree: evidence from tuition discontinuities. REStat.
  • Question: How does an increase in tuition affect the probability of late graduation?
  • Broader Question: tuition fees → academic outcomes
  • Policy: Tuition fees paid by a student is a function of real family income.
  • Outcomes: fuiro corso (late graduation)
  • Data Source: admin data: Bocconi University, Italy
  • Sample: students enrolling in the four-year economics program at Bocconi from 1992 to 1999 (12,127 students)
  • Identification: Fuzzy RD - threshold: tuition discontinuities determined by family income
  • Conclusion: A 1,000 euro increase in continuation tuition reduces the probablity of late graduation by 5.2%.
5. Malte Hübner. (2012). Do tuition fees affect enrollment behavior? Evidence from a ‘natural experiment’ in Germany. Econ. Edu. Rev.
  • Question: Do tuition fees affect enrollment behavior?
  • Broader Question: tuition fees → student behaviors
  • Policy: the introduction of tuition fees in seven of the sixteen German states in 2007
  • Outcomes: enrollment rates of highschool graduates
  • Data Source: admin data from the Federal Statistical Office of Germany (state-level, panel)
  • Sample: all German high-school graduates in all states in Germany, 2002 to 2008
  • Identification: Difference in difference - changed states vs unchanged ones before and after
  • Conclusion: The introduction of tuition fees at a level of roughly 1000 euros annually reduced enrollment rates among high school students by 2.7 percentage points.
    Using the structural model to correct for the spill-over to the comparison group suggests that the true effect has reduced enrollment probabilities by approximately 4.7 percentage points.
  • Side notes: To account for the spill-over effect from students' imperfect mobility among state, the results from the differencein-differences estimation are related to the parameters of a simple structural model of the enrollment decision.
6. Jeffrey T. Denning. (2012). College on the Cheap: Consequences of Community College Tuition Reductions. AEJ: Economic Policy.
  • Question: How do reductions in community college tuition fees affect enrollment decisions?
  • Broader Question: tuition fees → student behaviors
  • Policy: At least five community colleges in Texas have expanded their taxing district through annexation of municipalities, entitling students in the annexation to discounts in tuition fees ("in-district" vs "out-of-district" tuition fees)
  • Outcomes: enrollment, graduation rate
  • Data Source: admin data from Texas Education Research Center (student-level, panel)
  • Sample: students who graduated from Texas public high schools when 17 or 18 years old between 1994 and 2005
  • Identification: Differences in Differences - annexed districts vs districts already in a taxing district before and after annexation
  • Conclusion: 1) A $1,000 decrease in community college tuition increases immediate enrollment in community colleges by 5.1 percentage points relative to a baseline of 26.5 pp.
    2) Marginal community college enrollees induced to attend by reduced tuition have similar graduation rates as average community college enrollees
  • Side notes: sticker price rather than paid fees
7. Dynarski et al. (2021). Closing the Gap: The Effect of Reducing Complexity and Uncertainty in College Pricing on the Choices of Low-Income Students. AER.
  • Question: Can targeted, personalized communications, which reframe but do not increase financial aid, alter the college decisions of low-income students?
  • Broader Question: tuition fees → student behaviors
  • Outcomes: application, enrolment
  • Data Source: admin data from the Michigan Department of Education (longitudinal, student-level)
  • Sample: 2,108 students (qualified for subsidized school meals) from 529 schools for the first cohort, and 1,802 students from 497 schools for the second
  • Identification: RCT - Sending mailings to treated students to encourage them to apply to University of Michigan, and guarantee them of four years of free tuition and fees once admitted.
  • Conclusion: The intervention shifted low-income, high-ability students away from two-year and less selective four-year colleges. The gap in attending an institution considered “selective” or above between low-income and upper-income students was reduced from 12 percentage points to 4 percentage points, and the income gap in attending an institution considered “very selective” or above was eliminated.
  • Side notes: This is about some other properties of the fees, not the amount.
8. Murphy et al. (2019). The end of free college in England: Implications for enrolments, equity, and quality. Econ. Educ. Rev.
  • Question: Is it possible to charge relatively high rates of tuition fees and also protect (and even improve) these three goals, or do tuition fees stand in the way of these goals?
  • Broader Question: tuition fees → social outcomes
  • Policy Change: A progressive reform in England during two decades from 1998 - The shift moved England's higher education system from a free system to a high fee, high aid system, and shifted the costs of higher education away from the taxpayer and towards graduates themselves, with universities and students the key financial beneficiaries.
  • Outcomes: enrolments, access to higher education, institutional quality
  • Data Source: survey data - Labour Force Survey (for enrolments); admin data from the HESA
  • Sample: from 1992 to 2016
  • Identification: NA (descriptive statistics only!)
  • Conclusion: Following England's progressive reform starting from 1998,
    1) university enrolment has increased substantially, and
    2) participation from lower socio-economic groups has stabilized or even grown.
II. On Collge Costs Generally
9. Murphy and Wyness. (2023). Testing Means-Tested Aid. JOLE.
  • Question: Can financial aid be used to help equalize the outcomes of students once they are enrolled in college?
  • Broader Question: financial aids → academic outcomes
  • Policy: Institutional Financial Aid (IFA) - unexpected, means-test, no need to apply
  • Outcomes: first year completion rate, course scores, good degree (1st, 2:1) or not
  • Data Source: admin data: 9 English universities
  • Sample: students entering college between 2006/7 and 2009/10 (22,770 students)
  • Identification:
    1) OLS (with the “heavily parameterized regression design” - controling a smooth function of parental income to separate out the effect at discontinuities of the IFA) and
    2) IV (eligibility → amount of aid → outcomes)
  • Conclusion: Each £1,000 of IFA during the first year of college
    1) increases the chances of completing that year by 1.4 percentage points,
    2) improves test scores by 0.059σ, and
    3) increases the chances of graduating with a good-degree by 3.4 percentage points.
  • Heterogeneity: High ability and lowincome students benefit the most.
10. Denning et al. (2019). ProPelled: The Effects of Grants on Graduation, Earnings, and Welfare. AEJ: Applied Economics.
  • Question: How does eligibility for additional grant aid provided to low-income college students affect their academic and labor market outcomes?
  • Broader Question: financial aids → academic and labor market outcomes
  • Policy: Pell Grant aid - eligible students (family income lower than some threshold) are more likely to receive an additional aid of about $700.
  • Outcomes:
    1) short-run: credit hours attempted, persistence/2nd year enrolment, GPA
    2) long-run: graduation time, earnings afterwards
  • Data Source: admin data from Texas Higher Education Coordinating Board and Texas Workforce Commission
  • Sample: first-time-in-college and returning students who enrolled in a public four-year institution in Texas between 2008 and 2011.
  • Identification: Fuzzy RD - thershold: lowest family income
  • Conclusion:
    1) Eligibility for additional grant aid significantly increases degree completion and later earnings of first-time four-year university students.
    2) The government should fully recover the increase in grant expenditures within 10 years.
11. Castleman and Long. (2016). Looking beyond Enrollment: The Causal Effect of Need-Based Grants on College Access, Persistence, and Graduation. JOLE.
  • Question: Whether the eligibility for the need-based Florida Student Access Grant has an impact on students to enter, remain enrolled in, and graduate from college?
  • Broader Question: financial aid → academic outcomes
  • Policy: need-based grant Florida Student Access Grant
  • Outcomes: enrollment, persistence in first year, credits accumulated, completion
  • Data Source: admin data from K-20 Data Warehouse, Florida Department of Education (panel, student-level, secondary and post-secondary education)
  • Sample: seniors in Florida public high schools during the 1999–2000 academic year (101,094 studnets, of which 45,727 submitted the application to aid)
  • Identification: RD - threshold: family income (or "Expected Family Contribution") to determine eligibility for the grant
  • Conclusion: Every $1,000 of aid eligibility increases
    1) enrollment by 2.5 percentage points,
    2) persistence through freshman year by 3.3 percentage points,
    3) the cumulative number of credits students completed after 4 years by 3.4 credits, and
    4) the probability of completion within 5, 6, and 7 years by 2.5, 3.5, and 4.0 percentage points, respectively.

15. Demming and Dynarski. (2009). Into College, Out of Poverty? Policies to Increase the Postsecondary Attainment of the Poor. NBER WP.
  • Review paper:
    • on causality from college costs to educational attainment
    • summarizing key studies before 2010
  • Context: North America
  • Outcomes: enrollment, persistence, credits obtained, GPA
  • Two strands of studies: experiments; discrete shifts in aid policies (quasi-experiments)
    1. Experiments (small sample size): Upward Bound (1992-1994, US), STAR (2005, Canada), Opening Doors (2003, 2004)
    2. Policies: Pell Grant, veterans' educational benefits; subsidized public tuitions by states, merit-based state scholarship (e.g., HOPE in Georgia, ); private organization fundings (e.g., Gates Millenium Scholarship) etc.
  • Conclusion:
    1) Reducing costs increase entry and persistence - every $1000 can, among others:
        increase enrollment: by roughly 3-6 percentage points
        increase 1-3 credits obtained, and over 10 points in pass rate
        decrease dropout (or not): by 7-8 percentage points
    2) Program designs matter: Simple and transparent programs appear to be most effective.
