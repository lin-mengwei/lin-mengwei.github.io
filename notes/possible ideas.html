2. Re-Training Elderly Workers in a Aging World.

2. Re-Training Elderly Workers in a Aging World.(1)
  • Background: Our world is currently experiencing population aging. While the pace of aging may vary - developed countries in Europe and America being in the middle stage and developing countries such as China being in the early stages - all countries face similar challenges: decreases in the working-age population and an increase in social burden can cause the economic output growth to slow down, which, in turn, may further lead to potential conflicts in the distribution field, bringing economic recessions and social instability. The ageing society is a significant issue that must be addressed in all countries.
    Current research has focused on increasing the consumption of the elderly and reducing the burden of social elderly care. These studies recommend developing a "silver market" to target the consumption needs of the elderly and providing more elderly care as public services to reduce the pressure on young people to support their elders. These approaches are necessary but insufficiently, as they are mainly derived from the point of view that treat the elderly as unproductive piggy banks or social burdens. To build a harmonious and sustainable aging society, it is necessary to change our mindset. Elderly people are also able to contribute to socio-economic development with proper measures taken. (See the Employment of Older Workers in Europe and related research and data in Europe)
2. Revitalizing Human Capital for Elderly Workers in a Aging World. (2)
  • Motivation: One way to achieve this is to provide trainings to relatively old workers, allowing them to accumulate new human capital and reducing the depreciation rate of their human capital. This will make them more adaptable to today's rapidly changing work environment and improve their productivity.
