How do Retake Costs Change Student Behavior?




1    Introduction

Background: what has happened in the real world?

  • Higher education performance matters for both economies and individuals: R&D, economic development, labor market outcomes,...

  • Resource input is large in Higher Education (UNESCO, 2022)
    • Per-student spending in HE tops the education system around the world
    • HE funding keeps rising faster than enrolment

  • The efficiency could improve: In OECD countries, only 39% HE students graduate on time and over 1/5 do not graduate after all (OECD, 2022)

  • Incentivizing students to achieve better performance can improve the efficiency of HE resources and hence public financial investivents in HE.

1    Introduction: Research Question and Related Literature

Question: what do we want to understand? and why?

Does raising the tuition fees, especially the fees for retaking a course, incentivize students to work harder?

Literature: which academic conversations are we in?

  • Literature on incentives for academic performance
    1. non-financial incentives (Fidjeland, 2023; Jalava et al., 2015; Grove and Wasserman, 2006; Oettinger, 2002)
    2. financial incentives: reward-based (Behrman et al., 2015; Fryer, 2011; Angrist and Lavy, 2009) and penalty-based ones.
  • Literature on university costs, public investment in HE, and student outcomes
    1. the effects of grants and financial aid (Murphy and Wyness, 2023; Denning et al., 2019; Castleman and Long, 2016; Fryer, 2011)
    2. the effects of tuition fees (Bietenbeck et al., 2023; Murphy et al., 2019; Beneito et al., 2018; Garibaldi et al., 2012)

1    Does Raising Costs Boost Student Effort?

Identification Strategy (1): the "student-year" specification

Staggered diffrence-in-diffrerence: \[ y_{ics}=\sum_{c=2007}^{2017}\alpha_c+\sum_{s=1}^4\gamma_s+\beta D_{ics}+\theta X_i+\varepsilon_{ics}, \]
  • $\alpha_c$ and $\gamma_s$: cohort ($c$) and year-of-study ($s$) Fixed Effects; $y_{ics}$: outcome of student $i$ in the $k$-th year of study (e.g., average of credits taken, course passing rate, participation dummy);$D_{ics}$: treatment dummy taking 1 if $i$ in $c$ exposed to the rise in the $s$-th year of study

  • Control group: 2007 cohort--not exposed to the fee rise until the 5th year of study
  • Treated groups:
    1. 2008 cohort - exposed to the first rise in 2011 (4th year of study)
    2. 2009 cohort - exposed to the first rise in 2011 (3th year of study) and the second in 2012 (4th year of study)
